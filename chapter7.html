Chapter 7

Lies Among the Saints

Lies in the church are rarely obvious.

They are not always spoken outright.

They are implied. Assumed. Repeated until they feel true.

These lies often travel through gossip.

A story is shared with missing pieces. A concern is voiced without confirmation. A conclusion is reached without conversation. Over time, the repetition of an incomplete narrative becomes accepted reality.

This is how lies gain authority among believers.

The most damaging lies are not always about facts. They are about identity.

That person is dangerous. That person is compromised. That person cannot be trusted. That person will never change.

Once these ideas take root, they shape how people are treated. Distance feels justified. Exclusion feels wise. Silence feels spiritual.

Truth becomes secondary to perception.

The church often believes it is protecting itself when it entertains these lies. In reality, it is protecting comfort, reputation, and control. Lies allow people to avoid the discomfort of direct conversation. They remove the need for courage.

It feels safer to believe something about someone than to risk being wrong.

Lies among the saints also include what is left unsaid.

The truth about harm that occurred. The truth about leadership failures. The truth about systems that enabled abuse.

Silence becomes a lie when it hides injustice.

Many who were gossiped about later discovered that people believed things about them that were never true. By the time they realized it, the narrative was already established. Correcting it felt impossible. The damage was done.

This is where despair sets in.

When lies are believed by spiritual communities, they feel unmovable. People assume God must be behind the consensus. They begin to doubt their own reality. They wonder if they imagined the harm.

This kind of gaslighting is spiritually devastating.

Jesus is not the author of confusion.

He spoke truth clearly and directly. He corrected false narratives. He defended those misrepresented by religious leaders. He exposed hypocrisy even when it cost Him favor.

The saints are not immune to deception.

Good intentions do not guarantee truth. Spiritual language does not sanctify falsehood. Agreement does not equal discernment.

Lies flourish where humility is absent.

The willingness to ask, “What if we are wrong?” is essential to spiritual health. Without it, communities become echo chambers where gossip is reinforced and truth is silenced.

Lies also affect the one who believes them.

Participating in gossip dulls discernment. It trains the heart to judge without compassion. It distances people from the Spirit of Jesus, even while they believe they are defending righteousness.

Truth spoken without love becomes a lie.

If you have been crushed by lies told in holy spaces, hear this.

God knows what was said about you. He knows what was false. He knows what was withheld.

You are not defined by what people believed about you.

You are not bound by narratives you did not choose.

Truth has a way of resurfacing, even after long silence.

Jesus is committed to truth because truth heals.

This chapter is not meant to breed suspicion. It is meant to awaken discernment rooted in love. Lies lose power when light is welcomed, humility is practiced, and courage replaces convenience.

The church does not need more information.

It needs more integrity.

And for those wounded by lies among the saints, Jesus remains faithful. He is not confused about who you are.

He knows the truth.

And the truth is gentler than the lies ever were.
